With the growing number of uses on Artificial Intelligence (AI), this report will cover the impact of AI as a Disruptive Technology to Digital Art Commissions and the reason why it is considered as one.

Generative AI

Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI), also known as GAI, refers to systems that have the ability to create innovative content such as text, images, or music centered on patterns acquired from existing data. This technology has shown great promise in fields such as art and design, where AI can generate original pieces of music or create unique visual compositions. However, there are concerns surrounding the ethical implications of AI-generated content, particularly in terms of ownership and attribution. As AI continues to advance, it will be important for society to consider how to protect the rights of creators and ensure that proper credit is given to human artists.

One of the key advantages of Generative AI is its ability to assist artists and designers in the creative process. For example, tools like Adobe's Project Scribbler use AI to help designers generate new ideas and explore different visual styles. This can be especially useful for professionals who are trying to overcome creative blocks or broaden their artistic horizons. Through leveraging the power of AI, creators have access to a wealth of inspiration and resources that can help them push the boundaries of their work.

On the other hand, there are valid concerns about the potential for Generative AI to infringe on intellectual property rights. As AI systems become more sophisticated in generating original content, there is a risk that they may produce work that closely resembles existing copyrighted material. This could lead to legal disputes over ownership and authorship, as it may be difficult to determine whether a piece of AI-generated content was truly created by the machine or inspired by pre-existing works. As such, it will be crucial for legal frameworks to develop and adapt to address these growing issues in the area of AI-generated content.

In conclusion, Generative AI has the potential to revolutionize the way we approach creativity and innovation in various fields. While there are ethical considerations to be mindful of, such as ensuring proper attribution and protecting intellectual property rights, the benefits of AI in generating original content are vast. As technology continues to progress, it will be essential for creators, decision-makers, and society as a collective to work together to create guidelines and regulations that maintain ethical standards while exploiting the full potential of Generative AI.



Current situation of AI and digital artists

The history of artificial intelligence (AI) and digital art in particular is not extensive in terms of time as it only really gained massive popularity in 2022 with the release of multitudes of generative AI models such as Stable Diffusion. Not to get it confused with it being non-existent, just that; it was not as popular as it is now. In fact, early milestones and discoveries on this technology date back to the 1970s with Harold Cohen’s AARON, an AI that could generate artworks autonomously with a rule-based system and is what laid the foundation for what AI might be able to achieve in arts (Poltronieri & Hänska, 2019; Garcia, 2016).

By the 2010s, machine and deep learning techniques were introduced. In this case, Generative Adversarial Networks or GANs. This technique revolutionized the role of AI in art since it enabled the creation of much more complex and realistic images. This was a significant leap from the abstract outputs made by the earlier systems – their predecessors which relied on rules (Poltronieri & Hänska, 2019). These advancements began to blur the line between human and machine creativity. If these machines are already able to make complex outputs, what more in the future? By this point, AI-generated still remained mostly confined to an experimental and academic environment and not to the general public.

Generative AI began to make its waves and became a mainstream phenomenon in the year of 2022, with the release of platforms such as Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, and Midjourney. The existence of these tools and platforms allowed the democratization of access to AI-generated art, enabling users without any form of artistic training to create visually complex artworks with just simple text prompts (Cousins, 2023). This became a turning point in the relationship between AI and digital art, the start of a bad omen, as AI became not just an assistant or a tool to help in the creativity department but a producer of its own right, capable of creating its “own” art.

The abuse of AI in digital art

New avenues of creativity came to light with the rise of AI art tools. While that is true to some degree, it also led to widespread concerns about their misuse by bad actors. One of the most controversial ones is the use of existing, copyrighted materials to train AI models. Sadly, many of these generative AI systems rely on datasets that include works made by human artists, often without their consent or any compensation. This led to allegations of intellectual property theft or copyright infringement as these models often mimic the styles of the artists they were trained in (Han, 2023; Metz, 2022).

The lack of transparency from these companies with their AI models has left many artists feeling exploited and concerns about being replaced by these systems have begun to bore fruit. If the artists’ very own works are used to train these AI systems, what else would we be doing in the future? The absence of clear and definitive guidelines as generative AI only gained mainstream attention in recent years further exacerbates the problem, allowing these tech companies to profit while human artists suffer from a lack of a clear legal framework to protect their rights (Vyas, 2022).

AI as a disruptive technology for digital artists

Art has always reflected the human experience; it's a way to express our emotions, reflect the culture, and pursue creativity. In recent years, technology has entered the art world, and how it has turned artists' perception of art upside down.

Even when photography was invented, many artists did not accept it as art. Many photographers took their inspiration from paintings. In the present day, had photography emerged to this generation, it might seem to be a disruptive technology to artists, like how we see AI today.
Let's explore some of the main concerns about AI and the world of digital artists, including the implications, their rights, and how disruptive technology can work alongside art. Generative AI is undeniably great. It has revolutionized creative fields in many forms, including digital arts, by introducing different instruments and capabilities. The disruptive nature of artificial intelligence challenges traditional processes and raises alarms about the role of human artists in a constant revolution in the industry.

Creativity at scale and accessibility

With prompting, anyone can design high-quality logos, provided that an AI app supplies all necessary information or keywords. Most small businesses take advantage of this service from different AI apps to generate the artistic creation they need for their brand, from logos to labels to ideal branding. One good example of this is Dall-E.

Cost efficiency

Traditionally, businesses hire staff to operate. Larger businesses require a greater number of employees to run the business. With Zendesk's AI, automated bots can efficiently resolve common customer issues like password resets or order tracking. This significantly reduces staffing costs while ensuring faster response times, making it a cost-effective solution for businesses operating on tight budgets.

Generative AI disrupts the Digital Industry by devaluing artistic labor. Anyone who needs to be more creative can become an artist using AI technologies. The perception of art as a specialized skill is challenged when algorithms can effectively mimic these skills.

The shift in creative roles

For instance, creators are becoming curators. This was noted in the 1960s when the industry took "the curatorial turn."

Copyright and ethical challenges

Generative AI often trains on existing artworks without explicit permission, leading to debates about intellectual property. Digital artists may find their unique styles reproduced without credit or compensation.

Market saturation

The influx of AI-generated art risks oversaturating markets like stock imagery, NFT collections, and digital platforms. This abundance can make it harder for traditional artists to stand out.

Redefinition of originality

Generative AI blurs the line between original and derivative works. If an AI creates a piece inspired by thousands of existing artworks, who owns the creative rights? This question disrupts traditional views on authorship.

Opportunities amid disruption

  • Enhanced Productivity:Artists can use AI to prototype ideas or iterate quickly, saving time for higher-level creativity.

  • New Business Models: Artists can monetize their unique styles by training custom AI models for clients.

  • Expanding Creative Horizons: AI-generated works can inspire new approaches, combining human ingenuity with machine precision.

Generative AI’s disruption of digital art is a double-edged dagger. It democratizes originality, pushes artistic boundaries, and challenges artists' traditional roles, skills, and economic stability. Embracing this change requires adaptability and a willingness to redefine what it means to create in a world where AI is a co-creator.

5 Threats of generative AI to the digital art industry

Outputs lack emotional depth

AI-generated arts often miss certain emotions and personal touches that only human artists can provide upon creating their artworks. This emotional connection is what makes their artworks truly special. Therefore, if AI-generated images and illustrations are tolerated, they’ll face certain issues that will stain the reputations of digital artists being told by their clients that it is just easy to illustrate through generating AI by simply prompting any desired structure.\

Loss of unique artistic styles

AI can easily replicate and blend various artistic styles usually owned by digital artists and posted on different sites. As AI tools become more advanced, they can easily imitate the techniques of well-known artists leading to a homogenization of art styles. This increases the difficulty for artists in maintaining their unique artistic styles and standing out in a crowded market. When clients can access art that resembles established styles at the click of a button, the distinctive qualities that define an artist's work may be overshadowed, diminishing the value of originality in the art world.

Decreased income

The rise of AI-generated art has the potential to significantly impact the income of digital artists considering that as clients discover that they can obtain high-quality artwork at a lower cost through AI tools, they may choose these options over traditional/digital commissions. This shift could lead to fewer job opportunities for artists who only rely on commissions as their primary source of income. The financial strain on artists may affect livelihoods and discourage new talents and skills embodied by the new generation from entering the field, ultimately stifling creativity and innovation in the art community.

Copyright and plagiarism issues

The use of AI in creating various art styles raises serious concerns regarding the copyright and plagiarism of artworks produced using ibisPaint or any other applications. There are a lot of AI systems that are trained on vast datasets that include copyrighted works without the consent of the original artists. This leads to legal challenges, as artists argue that their ideas are being replicated without any compensation or acknowledgment from AI. Current lawsuits highlight the struggle to define fair use in the context of AI-generated art, as courts must determine whether these outputs are similar to other existing copyrights. The uncertainty surrounding ownership and rights over AI-generated images creates a risky environment for artists who may find their work devalued and unprotected in which technology can easily replicate their styles.

AI of the Future

What can we expect in the coming years?

Artificial intelligence (AI) has the potential to make far-reaching changes in the digital art landscape in the years to come. As AI algorithms become highly sophisticated, more realistic and high-quality AI art will be created. New AI tools and software will likely emerge, making the AI art creation process faster and more accessible to a greater audience. These tools can also enable personalization, which enhances the human experience and even extends to bringing collaboration between human artists to offer assistance, new ideas, and suggestions, opening up opportunities for innovation. While AI-powered tools can create these new and advanced sets of things in the long run, the long-term impact remains visible in the art industry, specifically for digital artists.

The Next Evolution of AI-Generated Art

So, what's next? Based on ongoing research, the future of AI art is expected to include several improvements, such as (What Is AI-Generated Art?, 2024):

  1. Improved Realism and Detail: With continuous advancements, AI-generated art may feature more detailed and realistic representations.
  2. Combination of Multiple Media Modalities: Future AI tools may combine text, images, audio, and other media modalities to create multimedia products.
  3. Enhanced Interactivity and Dynamics: AI-generated art will likely incorporate new dynamic features, improving human interaction and creating immersive experiences.
  4. Generative Design Across Industries: AI art may expand to various industries, providing creative solutions and applications for design purposes.
  5. Exploring New Aesthetics: AI art could challenge the traditional or existing notions of art by exploring new and unconventional forms of aesthetics.

Given these possibilities, AI can serve as a powerful tool. Still, it poses risks, particularly for digital art services and commissions. It could threaten the livelihood of digital artists and challenge our notion of art, especially for future generations. Indeed, AI art can be beneficial in its very own way. However, there should be boundaries and limitations, and they should not overshadow the artists' talents, capabilities, and ways of living.

Navigating ethical concerns in the future of AI art

AI-generated art presents various challenges and ethical concerns in the present day. Some current ethical issues surrounding AI art include ownership (intellectual property and rights), privacy, creativity and originality, biased representations, and questions about whether AI could replace a human (Perlman, 2024). These are just a few ethical concerns that need to be addressed. Therefore, regulations and guidelines should be implemented to protect human artists, creators, and their work in the future.

Art and AI

The field of art and the role of AI will continue to evolve in the future. Still, there will always be a significant difference between art created by humans and machines. The so-called “human-AI collaboration” may potentially grow in the coming years. But it’s important to remember that art is inherently free, and humans are not bound by standards when creating and expressing themselves through art. Moreover, the ability of artists and creators to add meaning and convey emotion in artwork is something that will remain unique and irreplaceable for humans. Indeed, “while AI may become a part of the creative process, it is the human element that will continue to define art” (AGI Fine Art Expert, 2024).

AI and its effects on the evolution of digital art styles

Erosion of traditional skills and techniques

The integration of AI into the creative process has led to the erosion of traditional skills and techniques in digital art. Traditionally, digital artists have invested years in mastering complex software and developing unique styles through painstaking practice. However, AI algorithms can now generate intricate and visually appealing artworks with minimal human intervention, bypassing the need for traditional artistic skills. This shift undermines the value of manual expertise and could potentially render long-developed techniques obsolete. As AI-generated art gains popularity, the demand for traditionally created digital art may decline, devaluing the skill and effort that artists have historically invested in their craft.

Homogenization of Art Styles

Given how we are at the cusp of AI integration into digital art, there seems to be a downward trend of having liberty and creativity shine out in the digital art landscape. Digital artists take pride in their unique creative voices and diverse artistic expressions, which contribute to the richness of the art community. However, AI-generated art often relies on large datasets to create new pieces, which can lead to repetitive patterns and less stylistic diversity. This homogenization makes it challenging for individual artists to stand out, as their unique styles are overshadowed by the mass production capabilities of AI. The result is a saturation of the market with art that lacks the distinctiveness of human-created work, diminishing appreciation for individual creativity.

Ethical concerns and authorship issues

The use of AI in art creation raises significant ethical concerns and questions about authorship and originality. Digital artists rely on clear authorship and copyright protections to safeguard their work and maintain professional integrity. However, AI-generated art blurs the lines of authorship, as it is created by algorithms that are mostly trained through numerous publicly available artworks by digital artists and the like. Issues such as the potential for AI to replicate an artist's style without permission and the unclear ownership of AI-generated artworks can undermine the value and integrity of human-created art. This ambiguity poses legal and ethical challenges that must be addressed to ensure that artists' rights are protected in an increasingly AI-driven art world.
